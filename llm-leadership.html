LLM Leadership

Treat LLM as a team to build and lead.

The work that holds up over days and many sessions does not come from a better prompt. It comes from directing the model the way you would direct people: you set the vision and the judgment, you run adversarial review, you keep the human in the loop. Leadership is the part that does not transfer to the tool.

Each piece here is the practice in the open: something you can read, run, or check for yourself.

The thesis

A team you have to build and lead

Drawn from twenty-two years building healthcare data platforms and eighteen months running an LLM as a team. It traces five phases, from treating the model as fancy search to leading it like a group of capable people who need direction. It is specific about what went wrong along the way: the thread that agreed with everything, the drafts that echoed the last thing said.

The teaching series

The Interns

A series on running the team well, one leadership practice and one technique per episode. "Red Team" is the current one. It walks through a simple practice. When the work is not landing the way you want, you bring in a second model for a fresh set of eyes and point it at the first model's work. The story shows adversarial review as a normal habit, the kind the model makes easy once you are directing it rather than taking its first answer. That first answer is often confident and wrong, and a single agreeable thread will hand it to you and call it finished.

The working tool

Ship a real deck this week

A free setup guide for one recurring job: the monthly team-meeting deck. You spend about twenty minutes wiring it up once, and after that the model gathers the updates and drafts the slides while you decide what the meeting is actually about. The guide is firm on the line that matters. The model finds and writes. You own the story. It is the fastest way to feel the difference between asking a tool for output and directing one.

The method, demonstrated

Watch the method work

Think of it as a casting call. One part, three actors reading the same lines, two of them fresh off the bus that week. You are in the director's chair. You give the same brief to each, watch the reads side by side, and coach toward the take that lands. The part goes to the best read, not the biggest name or the newest face. A cost breakdown runs alongside, so you can see what the performance was worth. The published post came out of exactly this, unchanged, and you can watch every take, including the ones that fell flat.
