Claude for Research — Learn to use the Stick

The most capable chat tool is the one that asks you to drive
Hugh McCutchen · v2.0 · June 2026 · ~2,000 words
Part One

The car nobody explained

Most chat tools are automatics. They do the ordinary jobs well, keep the risky controls out of reach, and will not let you stall. For most people, most of the time, that is the right car. With ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude you write one clear prompt, ask a follow-up or two in the same thread, and good work comes back. Long-running jobs are where this gets unglued.

For a lot of my work I am using these tools to build understanding of something new and complex. I know in advance that it will take many hours of back and forth over multiple hours or days. Many times my research work becomes the input to a writing exercise that again takes many hours or days or even weeks. This is very different from a single well defined or complicated specification or prompt that I expect to be executed in a single long session.

My kind of work ends the same way in most tools. The wheels start to slip in the turns and I swear I’m driving the same problem section that I already drove once or even twice before. Sometimes the engine misfires and gives nonsense or completely stalls. So you pull out a draft or pieces of the draft to a separate document and finish it by hand. You don’t see this nearly as much in Claude but it can end up in the same pattern unless you have really learned to drive it.

Most of what is written about getting more from Claude is aimed at developers. Token budgets, rate limits, prompt caching, agent orchestration. Useful for a coder, but not much help for someone using the tool for long research and writing. For someone like me, I mostly find feature tours and almost nothing on why those features matter for my work.

What drew me to Claude is that it did the normal tasks of reasoning so much better. Even more compelling was that it draws a clean diagram and produces a document that is formatted so nicely I didn’t have to spend hours cleaning up all the drafts before I could share them. Those are real wins for the work I do. But the tool has a big catch.

The capability is heavily metered. Every plan runs on a usage window, and the strongest setup drains it fastest. A limitless budget would make that someone else’s problem. In the chat window it is a wholly new problem I didn’t experience in the other tools. What initially felt like a cost/budget issue was actually a feature.

Claude was demanding I learn to drive. I had to learn to use a stick shift and not just drive in automatic. Learning how that worked and why it worked changed everything for me.

If you want to do long running complex research and writing with an LLM partner side by side each step of the way, you will benefit from understanding the framework in Claude. It is very raw and not simple but it is powerful and for me it was transformative.

Part Two

Choosing your gears and Loading the vehicle

Picking a model or gear is important and to do that you need to weigh the work in front of you. How complex is it? Is there a lot of ambiguity and judgement needed? Then you match that to what you can afford to spend on it. Model, effort, and context are how you spend.

Weigh it first

Before you touch a control, judge two things about the task. How complex is it: a quick lookup, or a layered synthesis with real uncertainty to work through. And what kind of thinking does it need: open-ended judgment, or defined execution against a settled plan. Those two answers set everything downstream.

What can you afford in your time window

If you are working in Claude Chat for hours at a time you need to think of all your work as operating within a budget. You are not billed per token (unless you blow your budget and buy usage credits). But you are given a budget of credits that draws down against two separate time windows. There is a rolling 5-hour session cap and a total weekly cap and both apply.

So your choice of what model you choose and how hard you work it needs some thought. The real judgment goes to the model with the deepest reasoning, and defined execution goes to the fastest capable model. Today that reads as Opus to frame and synthesize, Sonnet to draft and produce, Haiku for the trivial.

My own path here was not clean. I ran Pro on Opus by default, because Opus handled everything I threw at it. But when Opus doubled in token usage I was blowing through my 5-hour window in just a couple of hours. I changed my pattern to focus on Opus defining the work with Sonnet executing and was impressed. Sonnet was completely capable of executing the work and at far lower use of the credits in the window which meant I could work longer for my monthly fee.

Opus still earns its cost when the topic carries more layers of uncertainty to wade through. Effort tunes either arrangement, and it is the dial I reach for second. My default is running Sonnet or Opus on High. I’ll back off on Opus or Sonnet to medium to reduce token usage on a long thread but I rarely switch the model mid-thread because that essentially causes a reload of all the context. It’s like asking a new person into the team midstream and having them read everything before they join in. That costs resources and it also invites inconsistency in the thinking. Early in a thread that is fine. Late in a thread it’s probably best just to start a new thread.

Context is what you’re carrying in the vehicle

Weighing the work tells you what the top strengths of your model will be and also how fast you’re going to burn your budget. Context controls what facts we’re working with and what we’re holding in our memory for the thread. And here is something that changed while I was writing this. The way Claude handles a long thread is not what it was, and the new behavior makes the discipline matter more, not less.

Claude used to hold your whole thread, every turn, word for word, until you hit a hard stop. Now the stop is softer. When a thread gets near the context limit, Claude summarizes the early material and keeps going, and it tells you the moment it happens. Everything below that point is still the full thread, word for word.

That is more similar to the tools I left. They encourage you to hold open very long threads and assemble a working context turn by turn, and the platform is automatically trimming or compressing as you go, and that is the pattern that makes long complex work fall apart. On Claude you can directly manage what the model is holding.

With Claude you can choose where the compression happens and what survives it. A break you cut on purpose with a decision about what to carry forward results in better work.

Habit The Hazard The Protection What it costs
A clean break at every deliverable You don’t know what is being compacted or how. You can’t even know for sure what the context you are working with is. You decided the break point and what to carry forward. Fresh resources with your full context for all of your work. Being aware of thread exhaustion and saving a draft or section. Open a fresh thread, carry the file forward.
Close with a handoff note The model can start to get confused with its logic or what the problem is or where the focus is at any point. The next session opens on a few pages with full continuity instead of a hundred-thousand-token thread. Think about open items, next step. It can be done in a minute or two if you let Claude help you build it.
Keep the context focused by choosing it specifically for the thread or the task in the thread Assuming that just piling on more context and research is making the work richer and more accurate. The model is only holding in memory what you want it to be thinking about. The thread lasts longer and ultimately you get more done with better quality in your 5-hour window. Thinking about what context you need for each task in your work.

These habits are the essentials for long-running, iterative work that builds toward a deliverable.

For a standalone question, skip all three and just pick the best model for your budget.

Is the process overhead worth it for your work?

Managing your context closely, breaking work across threads, writing a handoff note at the end of every session. That is real work, and a skeptic is right to ask whether it pays.

Start with what it buys.

First, I get better quality in what is written when I get through multiple drafts and iterations. The tool isn’t forgetting what we’re doing or getting lost in its thinking.

From a human perspective with the research and writing process, when I write five thousand words in the normal way, I get attached to the sentences, and they make me stubborn. When Claude holds the drafts, my investment moves up to the ideas vs the exact phrasing. It sits in the decisions: what the piece argues, and the order a reader needs to follow it. Those survive any rewrite. The prose becomes the cheap layer, regenerated on demand from the judgment you already banked. You can reshape the whole thing with a few prompts and feel nothing, because nothing you valued got destroyed. I hold the big crafting edits for the final draft.

It also sets you up to imagine a pattern of writing where the work is stable, controlled and all changes recorded and the whole thing governed by a flexible plan. And that framework with Claude is helping keep the work on track not just giving you content to use or stand alone edits on your own work. This sort of tool will be incredibly helpful for writing requirements and building well structured sets of rules that evolve over time and can’t just drop ideas at random out of the work.

Honestly, nobody has built the right tool for this work yet. Claude chat is a raw tool you can use for almost anything. A software engineer has an IDE: one place that holds the project, tracks versions, runs tests, and keeps state. And there are thousands of people all figuring that out and writing about it. Even when I am very specifically researching Claude features for this paper I keep getting feedback that is for the Code tool.

Part Three

What does this look like exactly?

The next paper is the full framework I’ve come up with to drive successfully if you know you want an LLM as your partner to drive the long and winding road of research and writing. This is where I have started to meet that imagined pattern of controlled stable research work using the Claude Project framework as my IDE. It is still early days but there is a lot of promise. This paper and all of my papers are built this way now.

We’ll cover

  • Why you want to bolt on your own gauges
  • How to use the project framework
  • The parts of a project charter
  • How to build your context for the project
  • How management of context within and between threads
  • Starting to build out your skills

If you read this far I assume you’re trying to solve these same problems. So get in the driver’s seat and practice driving with the stick — the next paper will introduce the tools that let you drive much further over far trickier roads with Claude than you ever could with other chat tools.
